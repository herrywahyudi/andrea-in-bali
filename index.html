From Bucharest to Bali

A Romanian girl who fell head over heels for a tropical island.

THE STORY

I was 27 when I first landed in Bali. I'd booked a one-week holiday on impulse. I figured I'd tick it off the bucket list and go back to my normal life. That's not what happened.

Day four I rented a scooter and drove north with no plan. I ended up in Ubud by accident. I sat in a café above a rice terrace, ordered a coffee that cost €1.80, and didn't move for three hours. That's when Bali started making sense.

The first three days (the honest version)

I spent them in Kuta. I'm not proud of it. Kuta was Bali's first tourist strip and it shows — chaotic, overcrowded, and mediocre. It's the first place you land and the place I'd tell you to leave as quickly as possible.

The Bali I fell in love with has nothing to do with the Bali in travel brochures. It's quieter. Weirder. More spiritual. More real.

Why I moved (and why I stayed)

A year later I was back. Then again six months after that. On the fourth trip I looked at my return ticket, thought about my office in Bucharest, and didn't use it. Three years later I'm still here. I have a favourite warung where they start making my order when they see me park outside.

Why this website exists

Most travel content about Bali is either outdated, sponsored, or written by someone who visited for five days and is now an "expert." I wanted honest advice from someone who actually lives here.

Everything on this site I've personally experienced. If I wouldn't recommend it to my best friend, it doesn't exist here. That's the deal.

Ubud

The soul of Bali. Rice terraces, temples, and incredible food all in one valley.

ANDREA'S TAKE

Ubud is the place that made me fall completely in love with Bali. There's a calm here that's hard to explain. You wake up to the sound of gamelan music, have coffee while watching fog rise from the jungle valley, and somehow the rest of the world just stops existing.

If you're visiting Bali for the first time, spend at least 3 nights here. Skip the tourist traps — the swing, the floating breakfast. Just wander. The real Ubud is in the tiny warungs, the random temples you stumble into, and the conversations with locals you weren't expecting to have.

  • 1

    Tegallalang Rice Terraces

    Go before 8am. Skip the swings. The terraces themselves are free to walk through.

  • 2

    Pura Tirta Empul Holy Springs

    The holy water temple. One of the most genuinely spiritual places in Bali. Sarong and sash provided at the gate.

  • 3

    Campuhan Ridge Walk

    9km through jungle and rice fields. Free. Magical early morning. Bring water.

  • 4

    Sacred Monkey Forest

    Yes they will steal your sunglasses. Keep belongings close. Don't show food. It's chaotic and completely worth it.

  • 5

    Kecak Dance at Uluwatu (Day Trip)

    Fire dancers at sunset over a 70m cliff. One of the most memorable things I've seen in my life.

  • 6

    Balinese Cooking Class

    Starts with a market tour, ends with you eating your own nasi goreng. About €35–55.

  • ☕

    Café Pomegranate

    Rice field views, best cold brew in Bali. Before 10am or expect a queue.

  • ☕

    Seniman Coffee

    Serious specialty single-origin coffee. Possibly the best espresso in Bali.

  • 🍜

    Warung Babi Guling Pak Malen

    Suckling pig rice. Life-changing sambal. Get there before 11am — they run out every day.

  • 🌿

    Moksa Plant-Based

    Grows everything in their permaculture garden. Even if you eat meat, go once.

  • 💎

    Komaneka at Bisma — Luxury

    Infinity pool over jungle canopy. Spectacular breakfast. From €280/night.

  • ✦

    Bisma Eight — Mid Range

    Infinity pool, rice field views, great breakfast. Best value in Ubud. From €130/night.

  • ○

    Alaya Resort — Budget

    Pool, central location, lovely staff. From €55/night.

Uluwatu

Clifftop temples, legendary surf breaks, and sunsets that stop your heart.

ANDREA'S TAKE

Uluwatu is the dramatic, slightly wild cousin of Bali. The cliffs here are 70 metres above the Indian Ocean and the Kecak dance at sunset is genuinely one of the most moving performances I've witnessed anywhere. Fire dancers, 50 men chanting in unison, the sun dropping into the ocean. Go.

  • 1

    Kecak Dance at Sunset

    Arrive 30 minutes early. Watch the sun drop into the ocean while fire dancers perform. Bring a sarong.

  • 2

    Padang Padang Beach

    Squeeze through a gap in the rocks to get there. One of the most beautiful small beaches I've been to anywhere on earth.

  • 3

    Single Fin Bar

    Best bar to watch the Uluwatu surf break. Cold Bintang, front row to world-class waves.

  • 4

    Jimbaran Fish Market Dinner

    Pick your fish from the market, they grill it on the beach. Watch the sunset over the ocean. Peak Bali.

Canggu

Digital nomads, rice fields, great coffee, and a very specific type of Bali energy.

ANDREA'S TAKE

Canggu is polarizing. Half the people who visit fall completely in love with it. The other half find it too Instagram-ified. I understand both reactions. The truth is Canggu has genuinely great cafés, good surf, and a creative energy that's hard to find anywhere else. The traffic is a nightmare. But there's a reason people end up staying much longer than planned.

  • 1

    Echo Beach Sunset

    Sit at any beach bar and watch the sun go down while surfers are still in the water. This is Canggu at its best.

  • 2

    Morning Coffee Crawl

    Tanamera → Hungry Bird → Cinta. Three cafés, three different vibes, all walkable.

  • 3

    Finn's Beach Club

    Over-the-top? Yes. Worth it for one afternoon? Also yes. Don't go on weekends.

Nusa Penida

The island that reminds you why you came to Bali in the first place.

ANDREA'S TAKE

Nusa Penida is the most dramatic place I've ever been. The roads are terrible, the weather can be brutal, and Kelingking Beach is one of those places where you struggle to believe this is real. Go. Just go.

  • 1

    Kelingking Beach (T-Rex Cliff)

    The viewpoint alone is extraordinary. If you're fit and brave, hike down to the beach. Steep, rough, incredible.

  • 2

    Angel's Billabong

    Natural infinity pool carved by the ocean into rock. Go at low tide.

  • 3

    Manta Ray Snorkeling

    One of the world's best manta ray spots. Book early morning.

  • 4

    Crystal Bay

    Most reliably beautiful beach on the island. Calm water, good snorkeling, lovely sunset.

Seminyak

Beach clubs, world-class restaurants, and Bali's most beautiful sunsets.

ANDREA'S TAKE

Seminyak is Bali's most cosmopolitan neighbourhood. The restaurants here are genuinely excellent. More polished than Canggu, more adult than Kuta, and the shopping on Jalan Laksmana is genuinely fun.

  • 1

    Sunset at Ku De Ta

    The original Bali beach club. Get there at 5pm, order a cocktail, watch the sun go down. Classic for a reason.

  • 2

    Dinner at Merah Putih

    Most beautiful restaurant building in Bali. Elevated Indonesian food. Book 2 days ahead.

  • 3

    Shopping on Jalan Laksmana

    Independent boutiques, local designers, beautiful homeware.

Sidemen

The most beautiful valley in Bali that most tourists have never heard of.

ANDREA'S TAKE

Sidemen is my favourite place in Bali that I almost never tell anyone about. And then I feel guilty and tell everyone. It's a wide valley in east Bali, framed by terraced rice paddies and the enormous silhouette of Mount Agung. Almost no tourists. Guesthouses are cheap. The food is real local cooking.

Come here for at least one night. Stay in a rice field guesthouse, wake up before dawn, sit on your balcony, and watch the mountain appear as the mist clears.

  • 1

    Wake Up Before Sunrise

    Set your alarm for 5am. Watch Mount Agung materialize from the mist. One of the most beautiful natural moments I've witnessed anywhere on earth.

  • 2

    Walk Through the Rice Fields

    No guides, no tour, no payment. Just walk. Follow the paths through the paddies.

  • 3

    Traditional Weaving Villages

    The women of Sidemen are famous for their traditional ikat weaving. Visit a family workshop — you can buy directly from the weavers.

  • 4

    River Swimming

    The Unda River runs through the valley. Ask locals to point you to the best swimming spot.

Amed

Black sand beaches, a shipwreck, and the most honest local feeling left in Bali.

ANDREA'S TAKE

Amed is three hours from the airport and three decades from the Bali most people visit. Black volcanic sand beaches. Traditional fishing villages. The USS Liberty shipwreck sitting in 30m of water just off the shore at Tulamben. Spectacular snorkeling and diving. Almost no traffic. A quietness that's increasingly hard to find on this island.

  • 1

    USS Liberty Shipwreck, Tulamben

    A WWII cargo ship in 5–30m of water, completely encrusted with coral. You can snorkel the shallower sections without diving experience.

  • 2

    Sunrise Over Mount Agung

    Wake up early and watch the volcano catch the first light of day with the black sand beach and fishing boats going out.

  • 3

    Snorkel from the Shore

    The coral gardens at Jemeluk Bay are particularly good and completely free to access.

  • 4

    Local Fishing Village Walk

    Walk through the fishing villages north of the main strip. Salt farms, traditional boats being repaired. Genuine local life.

Bali Travel Guide

Everything you need to know before you arrive. The practical stuff nobody blogs about.

THE ESSENTIALS

Andrea's complete Bali guide

Getting there & visas

Ngurah Rai International Airport (DPS). From Europe, 16–20 hours with one connection — Singapore, KL, and Doha are the most common hubs. Book 3+ months ahead. Most nationalities get a free 30-day visa on arrival, extendable once. Indonesia introduced the Bali Tourism Levy in 2024 — IDR 150,000 (~€9) per person. Keep your receipt.

🛵

Getting Around

Rent a scooter (~€5/day). Hire a driver for day trips (~€35–50). Use Grab for local taxis. Avoid airport exchange — rates are terrible.

💰

Money

Indonesian Rupiah (IDR). BCA and Mandiri ATMs have the best rates. Always carry cash — small warungs and markets are cash-only.

📱

SIM Card

Buy a Telkomsel SIM at the airport. ~€5 for 30 days of data. Essential for Grab, Google Maps, WhatsApp.

🌡

Weather

Dry season May–October. Wet season Nov–April — cheaper, fewer tourists. My favourite: September/October.

🏥

Health

No required vaccinations. Dengue is real — use DEET. BIMC Kuta is the best private hospital. Never drink tap water.

🕌

Culture & Respect

Cover shoulders and knees at temples. Step over (never on) offerings on the ground. Ask before photographing ceremonies.

🌊

Ocean Safety

Strong rip currents exist. Swim between flags. Kuta and Seminyak have lifeguards. Always ask locals before swimming at unfamiliar beaches.

🎒

What to Pack

Light clothing only. Reef-safe sunscreen. A sarong. Packable rain jacket for wet season. 40L backpack is enough.

Before You Go — 10 things to sort

1

Book airport transfer in advance

Do it before you land. Negotiating taxi prices while jet-lagged and surrounded by touts is not how you want to start your Bali trip.

2

Download Grab before you fly

The ride-hailing app for Southeast Asia. Set up your account at home while you have stable WiFi.

3

Get travel insurance

Medical evacuation from Bali is expensive. Get insurance that covers it. Safety Wing and Heymondo are good for longer stays.

4

Notify your bank

Tell them you're traveling to Indonesia or your card may get blocked at the first ATM. Takes 2 minutes.

5

Book at least your first two nights

Arrive with a place to sleep sorted. Landing without accommodation after a 20-hour journey is a bad start.

6

Save offline Google Maps

Download the Bali region before you fly. Mobile data works most of the time but rural areas can be spotty.

7

Buy reef-safe sunscreen now

Regular sunscreen is banned in many dive and snorkeling areas. Buy reef-safe before you go — it's cheaper in Europe than in Bali.

8

Pack a sarong

You need one for temples. It doubles as a beach towel, a picnic blanket, and a light blanket on the plane.

9

Learn three words of Indonesian

"Terima kasih" (thank you), "selamat pagi" (good morning), "berapa harga?" (how much?). Worth 10 minutes of your time.

10

Message Andrea

Her WhatsApp is always open for trip questions. She doesn't earn commission. She just loves helping people have a better Bali trip.

HONEST TAKES

Things I wish I knew before moving to Bali

Andrea·June 2025·8 min read

I moved to Bali three years ago after a long weekend trip that basically short-circuited my brain. I flew home to Bucharest, handed in my notice, and started Googling "how to move to Bali." Here's what I learned.

The traffic is actually insane

The roads in southern Bali can turn a 3km journey into a 45-minute ordeal. Rent a scooter and learn to drive it confidently. Avoid Kuta. Live north of Canggu if you want any quality of life.

The best version of Bali is the one you find when you stop trying to see everything and just start living somewhere properly.

Ubud is not Bali. Canggu is not Bali.

The places most tourists visit are tourist infrastructure built on top of a deeply spiritual and complex culture. The real Bali is in the temple ceremonies at 5am, in the offerings on every doorstep, in the warmth of someone who offers you food anyway.

It's cheaper than you think — until it isn't

A warung meal costs €1.50. A brunch at a trendy café costs €25. Both exist in Bali. Know which Bali you're choosing.

The spiritual element is not a performance

Bali is a Hindu island. The religion permeates everything. Treat it that way and Bali will give you something very hard to find anywhere else.

TOURIST TRAPS

5 places in Bali I'd skip (and what to do instead)

Andrea·May 2025·6 min read

This is going to upset some people. But you came here for honesty, not validation.

1. The Tegallalang Swing

It's a swing over a rice terrace. You pay 150,000–400,000 rupiah for a photo that looks identical to 47 million others. Skip it. Walk along the actual rice terrace instead — free and genuinely beautiful.

2. The Bali Floating Breakfast

In reality: the pool is cold, the food gets wet, and you spend your whole time arranging it for the camera. Spend that money on actual breakfast at a proper café instead.

Bali's most overrated experiences exist almost entirely for Instagram. The most memorable ones exist almost entirely in real life.

3. Kuta

Overcrowded, noisy, mediocre. The only reason to go to Kuta is if you're catching an early flight. Land, sleep one night, leave the next morning.

4. Mount Agung (unless very prepared)

3,031 metres, active volcano. If you want a volcano sunrise, do Mount Batur instead — easier, cheaper, equally spectacular.

5. Tanah Lot between 4–6pm

Genuinely beautiful temple. Go at 7am instead. Completely different experience.

MONEY

Is Bali actually expensive? The honest breakdown.

Andrea·April 2025·5 min read

Short answer: it depends entirely on how you travel.

Budget traveler — €30–45/day

Entirely doable. Guesthouse €15–25/night, warungs (€1.50–3 per meal), scooter (€5–7/day). A warung nasi goreng with egg costs about €1.20.

Mid-range — €80–150/day

Nice hotel with pool (€60–120/night), good restaurants half the time, organized activities.

The expensive version of Bali and the cheap version exist on literally the same streets.

Luxury — €250+/day

Private villas, fine dining, private drivers. This version of Bali is actually excellent value versus similar luxury in Europe.

SUNSETS

Best sunsets in Bali — ranked by someone who's seen a lot of them

Andrea·Jan 2025·4 min read

Three years of chasing Bali sunsets. Here's the ranking.

1. Uluwatu during Kecak — The one

70-metre cliff, 1,000-year-old temple, fire dancers performing as the sun drops into the Indian Ocean. The most beautiful thing I've seen in Bali. I've seen it 12 times. Never gets old.

2. Seminyak Beach

The long flat beach turns completely gold at sunset. Beach clubs, surfers, local kids playing football on the sand. Everyone silently experiencing the same thing together.

3. From a rice field in Ubud

Any rice field looking west as the light changes. The terraces glow. Fog rises. The sound is birds and wind and distant gamelan.

4. Tanah Lot at 7am

Go at 7am, before the tour buses. The temple in the mist is genuinely beautiful.

SOLO TRAVEL

What it's actually like to be a solo woman in Bali

Andrea·Dec 2024·6 min read

Is Bali safe for solo female travelers? Here is my completely honest answer after three years of living here.

The safety question

Yes. Genuinely. Bali is one of the safer destinations in Southeast Asia for solo women. The Balinese are warm, respectful, and genuinely helpful. In three years I have never felt unsafe. Not once.

Moving to Bali alone was the best decision I've made in my life. I am not the person I was before I came here. Bali changes you, and especially women who come alone.

The social side

Bali is extraordinarily easy to meet people in. The café and co-working culture in Canggu and Ubud means you'll naturally end up in conversation within hours of arriving.

The things nobody tells you

You will be a different person in Bali than you are at home. Women I've spoken to consistently describe it as a place where they felt permission to be exactly who they are.

CULTURE

The Bali I didn't expect — and why it changed everything

Andrea·Nov 2024·9 min read

When I first planned my trip to Bali I was thinking: beaches, rice terraces, good coffee. What I wasn't expecting was to feel, within a week, that I was touching something ancient and alive.

The offerings

Every morning, the Balinese make canang sari — small woven offerings of banana leaf filled with flowers, rice, and incense. Placed everywhere: on doorsteps, at road junctions, in shops. Every. Single. Day. These aren't decorative. They aren't for tourists. They're a daily act of gratitude that has been happening in Bali for over a thousand years.

The Bali that changes you isn't the Bali in the photos. It's the Bali you find when you slow down enough to actually watch what's happening around you.

The ceremonies

I once stumbled into a cremation ceremony while trying to find a café. I watched for about an hour. Hundreds of people dressed in white and yellow, gamelan music, enormous paper towers being carried through the streets. Everyone was calm. Even joyful. I cried, and I had no idea why.

What to do with this

You don't need to become spiritual. You just need to slow down and pay attention. Put the phone down for ten minutes in a temple. Sit quietly in a rice field. The best of Bali reveals itself to the people who stop rushing.

FAQ

Questions Andrea gets asked a lot

The most common questions from her WhatsApp inbox, answered honestly.

  • Is Bali safe for solo female travelers? ↓
    Yes, genuinely. I moved here as a solo woman and have never felt unsafe. The Balinese are warm and respectful. Take normal precautions — use Grab rather than walking alone late at night. But overall Bali is one of the safer destinations in Southeast Asia for solo women.
  • What's the best time of year to visit Bali? ↓
    May to October is dry season — best weather, least rain. July and August are peak season. November to April is wet season — cheaper, fewer tourists. My personal favourite: September/October.
  • Do I need to speak Indonesian? ↓
    No. English is widely spoken in tourist areas. Learning a few words goes a long way — "terima kasih" (thank you) and "selamat pagi" (good morning) will get you big smiles everywhere.
  • Should I rent a scooter? ↓
    If you're comfortable on a scooter and have experience — absolutely yes. It's the best and most flexible way to explore. ~€5–7/day. If you've never ridden one, don't learn in Bali traffic. Hire a driver instead.
  • Is tap water safe to drink? ↓
    No. Never drink tap water in Bali. Use bottled water or refill stations. Buy a reusable bottle and refill it — Bali has a serious plastic problem and every bit helps.
  • How much cash should I bring? ↓
    ATMs are everywhere in tourist areas. BCA and Mandiri ATMs have the best rates. Avoid airport currency exchange — rates are terrible. Have the equivalent of €100–150 in rupiah on you at all times. Small warungs, local markets, and scooter rental shops are still cash-only.
  • Can I use Uber or Lyft in Bali? ↓
    No Uber or Lyft. Use Grab — works exactly the same way and is reliable in all tourist areas. Gojek is the Indonesian equivalent and also works well. In more remote areas like Amed or Sidemen, hire a local driver.
  • What should I wear at temples? ↓
    Cover shoulders and knees. Most temples provide sarongs at the entrance. You'll also need a sash tied around your waist — always provided. Just accept the sarong at the gate.
  • How do I get from Bali to Nusa Penida? ↓
    Fast boat from Sanur Beach. Maruti Express and Seagull Fast Cruise are reliable. Takes about 45 minutes. Costs around IDR 150,000 (≈ €9) each way. Boats run from early morning to mid-afternoon.
  • What's Andrea's honest opinion of Kuta? ↓
    I don't recommend it. Kuta was Bali's first tourist area and it shows — overcrowded, noisy, mediocre. The only reason to go to Kuta is if you're catching an early flight. Land, sleep one night, leave the next morning.
  • How many days do I need in Bali? ↓
    Minimum 7 days to actually see and feel Bali properly. 10 days if you want to add Nusa Penida. 2 weeks if you want to go north. Don't try to see everything — pick 2-3 areas and go deep.
  • Is it rude to bargain in Bali? ↓
    At markets and street vendors — bargaining is expected and completely normal. Start at about 50–60% of the asking price. At restaurants, spas, hotels, and tour operators — don't bargain. Prices are fixed. Know where you are.
